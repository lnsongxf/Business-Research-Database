Article

The flexible workplace: What have we learned?

Authors

  • Charles S. Rodgers

    Search for more papers by this author

    • Charles Rodgers is a principal of Work/Family Directions in Boston, Massachusetts, and President of Rodgers 6 Associates, its consulting arm. Mr. Rodgers is trained as a labor economist and has been studying the impact of changing demographics at the workplace for over 15 years. He has advised leading corporations in the development of work and family strategies and conducted research on the changing needs of employees with family responsibilities. Prior to joining Work/Family Directions, Mr. Rodgers was a Vice-president at State Street Bank 6 Trust Company in Boston.

Abstract

Data collected on white-collar and services sector jobs in over 20 large corporations show both the enormous demand from employees for more flexibility in the workplace and the considerable resistance to it by supervisors. The interest in more flexible work practices stems from the tremendous time constraints faced by the many employees with significant family responsibilities. Current practices, schedules, and management philosophy are no longer adequate for workers in this important segment of the labor force and impede them from being as productive as they could be. The major barriers and enablers to flexibility are discussed, and the data on benefits are reviewed. It is argued that if progress toward a more flexible workplace is to continue, the emphasis will have to shift from a strategy of accommodating valued employees to new ways to manage and organize how work is done. © 1993 by John Wiley & Sons, Inc.
