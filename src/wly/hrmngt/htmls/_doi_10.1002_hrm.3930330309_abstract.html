Article

When promises become contracts: Implied contracts and handbook provisions on job security

Authors

  • Judi McLean Parks,

    Search for more papers by this author

    • Judi McLean Parks is an assistant professor of Organizational Behavior at the University of Minnesota's Industrial Relations Center. She is currently a visiting scholar at Cornell University. Her research interests include psychological and implied contracts and the changing employment relationship. Her research has appeared in journals such as the Journal of Applied Psychology and Academy of Management Journal. Her book chapters include: Till death us do part: The changing nature of organizational contracts and commitments (with D. Kidder), The contracts of individuals and organizations (with D. Rousseau), and Organizational contracting: A “rational” exchange? (with f. smith).

  • Deborah A. Schmedemann

    Search for more papers by this author

    • Deborah A. Schmedemann is a professor of law at William Mitchell College of law in St. Paul, Minnesota, where her interests include employment, labor and public sector law, as well as business ethics and the legal process. She is a coauthor of one of the foremost legal research texts, The process of legal research. Her research has appeared in publications such as the William Mitchell Law Review, the Virginia Law Review, the Industrial Relations Law Journal, and the Harvard Journal on Legislation. Professor Schmedemann earned her law degree from Harvard.

Abstract

In this article, policy capturing was used to examine how lawyers, as experts, understand the wording in employee handbook passages that promise procedural protections for employees. Specifically, how the wording of employee handbook passages can create both legal and nonlegal obligations was examined. Forty-six lawyers were asked to make the following judgments: whether the handbook passage constituted a legal or moral obligation and the chances of the employer winning or being granted a summary judgment in a wrongful discharge suit. Five aspects of the handbook passages were examined: verb strength, specificity, contract disclaimers, legalistic jargon, and the employee's signature.
