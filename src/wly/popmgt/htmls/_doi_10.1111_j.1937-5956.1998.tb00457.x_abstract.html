INSIGHTS ON SERVICE SYSTEM DESIGN FROM A NORMAL APPROXIMATION TO ERLANG'S DELAY FORMULA

Authors

  • PETER J. KOLESAR,

    1. Graduate School of Business, Columbia University, New York, New York 10027, USA

      Peter J. Kolesar is a professor in the Management Science/Operations Management Division of the Graduate School of Business, Columbia University, where he is also the Research Director of the Deming Center for Quality Management. He won the 1975 Lanchester Prize of ORSA and the 1976 Nato Systems Science Prize for his research on the optimal deployment of urban police patrol and fire fighting resources. Much of his current research is on generating approximate solutions for nonstationary queues. He is currently testing the accuracy of the workforce scheduling systems that are frequently used in telephone call centers. Professor Kolesar's other research work is focused on increasing the effectiveness of TQM implementation. He has twice been an examiner for the Malcolm Baldrige National Quality Award. Professor Kolesar has been a consultant to many firms in the United States and abroad on issues concerning quality and productivity. He has served on the editorial boards of Operations Research, Management Science, and Interfaces, and he is the author of some 50 technical papers on a broad range of topics in management science. Peter Kolesar may be reached at 408 Uris Hall, Columbia University, New York, NY 10027. Telephone: (212) 854–4105. Fax:(212) 316–9180. E-mail: pjk4@columbia.edu.

      Linda V. Green is the Armand G. Erpf Professor of Business in the Management Science/Operations Management Division of Columbia Business School. She holds an M.S. in Mathematics from the Courant Institute of New York University and a Ph.D. in Operations Research from Yale University. Before getting her doctorate, she worked for several years at AT&T Bell Laboratories as a member of the technical staff and then at AT&T corporate headquarters. She has done extensive research in the theory, modelling, and application of stochastic processes. Her work in queueing systems in which arrivals require a random number of servers formed the foundation of a police patrol car allocation model (called PCAM) that is used by most major cities in the United States as well as in other countries. Over the last several years, her research has focused on characterizing, predicting, and managing the behavior of service systems with time-varying demands. Other recent research has included work on the identification and investigation of factors most adversely affecting health care costs in the U.S.A. Current interests and efforts focus on assessing operations strategies in hospitals and, more specifically, on the development of a managerial framework for assessing the relationships and tradeoffs among capacity, costs, and service performance in hospitals. She is the author of numerous publications in the major journals in operations research and applied probability and until recently served as a Departmental Editor for Applied Stochastic Models of Management Science. She has served as a Council Member of TIMS and as Secretary/Treasurer of the ORSAI TIMS College of Applied Probability.

Abstract

We show how a simple normal approximation to Erlang's delay formula can be used to analyze capacity and staffing problems in service systems that can be modeled as M/M/s queues. The numbers of servers, s, needed in an M/M/s queueing system to assure a probability of delay of, at most, p can be well approximated by s ≅ p + z***I-p+, where z1-p, is the (1 - p)th percentile of the standard normal distribution and ρ, the presented load on the system, is the ratio of Λ, the customer arrival rate, to μ, the service rate. We examine the accuracy of this approximation over a set of parameters typical of service operations ranging from police patrol, through telemarketing to automatic teller machines, and we demonstrate that it tends to slightly underestimate the number of servers actually needed to hit the delay probability target—adding one server to the number suggested by the above formula typically gives the exact result. More importantly, the structure of the approximation promotes operational insight by explicitly linking the number of servers with server utilization and the customer service level. Using a scenario based on an actual teleservicing operation, we show how operations managers and designers can quickly obtain insights about the trade-offs between system size, system utilization and customer service. We argue that this little used approach deserves a prominent role in the operations analyst's and operations manager's toolbags.
