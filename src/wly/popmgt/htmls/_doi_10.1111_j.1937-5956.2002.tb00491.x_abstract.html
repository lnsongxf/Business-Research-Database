COMPONENT COMMONALITY: MODELS WITH PRODUCT-SPECIFIC SERVICE CONSTRAINTS*

Authors

  • PRAKASH MIRCHANDANI,

    1. Katz Graduate School of Business, University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania 15260 USA
    Search for more papers by this author
    • 3

      Prakash Mirchandani is Associate Professor of Business Administration at the Katz Graduate School of Business, University of Pittsburgh. He has a bachelors degree in Mechanical Engineering from the Indian Institute of Technology, an MBA from the Indian Institute of Management, and a Ph.D. in Management from the Sloan School of Management, MIT. Dr. Mirchandani has published in leading academic journals in the Management Science and Operations Management field, including Management Science, Operations Research, and Mathematical Programming, and is on the editorial board of The INFORMS Journal on Computing and of Telecommunication Systems. He was awarded the 1989 Zenon S. Zannetos prize for the best Ph.D. thesis at the Sloan School of Management. His research interests include capacitated network design for the transportation and telecommunication industries, heuristic analysis, and the impact of commonality on supply chain efficiency. Dr. Mirchandani teaches courses in Probability and Statistics, Decision Technologies in Manufacturing and Operations Management, Distribution Networks and Supply Chain Management. He was awarded the 1999 and the 2001 Outstanding Professor Award by the Katz full-time MBA students, and has been the runner-up for this award several times in the past. He was also a finalist for the 199697 and the 20012002 University of Pittsburgh Chancellors Distinguished Teacher Award. He has consulted for several clients and conducted projects, among others, on process improvement, demand forecasting, and decision support system development.

Abstract

Common components are used extensively for reasons including product postponement and expediting new product development. We consider a two-stage assemble-to-order system with two products having uniformly distributed demand, one common component, and product-specific components. We develop optimization models in which the cost-minimizing inventory of the components must be determined and allocated to products in order to meet product-specific service level constraints. We compare two different commonality models based on whether or not the products are prioritized. A distinctive feature of our study is the use of product-specific service levels. We compare our results with models using aggregate service levels.
